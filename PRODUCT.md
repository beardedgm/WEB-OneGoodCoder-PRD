# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Hiring managers, recruiters, and prospective clients or collaborators evaluating Derrick Dedmon for communications and creative-leadership roles or project work. They usually arrive from a LinkedIn profile, a referral, or a job application, often on a quick first pass, and they are deciding one thing: is this person credible and worth a conversation? Many will skim on a phone between other tasks; some will want to print or save the resume.

## Product Purpose

A single-page personal resume and portfolio for Derrick Dedmon. It presents his 25+ year career arc (graphic design → web development → communications leadership), his capabilities, and selected work, in a form that reads quickly, holds up to scrutiny, and supports the next step: a message, a call, or a printed resume. Success is a visitor who leaves confident in his experience and takes that next step. It is a communication surface, not a transactional app.

## Brand Personality

Warm, editorial, credible. The voice is human and assured: plainspoken sentences, first-person narrative, no jargon and no salesmanship. It should feel like a well-set magazine feature about a career, written by someone with nothing to prove and the record to back it up. Confidence is shown through the work and the prose, never through hype.

## Anti-references

- **Generic SaaS / startup landing pages** — gradient hero, purple-on-white, the big-number hero-metric template, endless identical icon-card grids.
- **Corporate LinkedIn / resume-builder templates** — stock photography, default-blue corporate palette, soulless layouts with no point of view.
- **Dark developer-terminal aesthetic** — neon-on-black, monospace-everything, code-editor cosplay. Wrong register for a communications leader.
- **Flashy / maximalist** — parallax overload, heavy animation, motion that upstages the content.
- **AI slop** — anything that reads as machine-generated default: timid palettes, average layouts, the tells a visitor would clock instantly as "made by AI."

## Design Principles

- **Earned authority over self-promotion.** Let the 25-year track record and the prose carry the credibility. No buzzwords, no superlatives, no hype.
- **Warmth without softness.** Approachable and human, but never timid or vague. The cream-and-terracotta palette is friendly; the hierarchy and copy are decisive.
- **Editorial restraint.** Magazine clarity: generous space, strong typographic hierarchy, one idea per section, deliberate pacing.
- **Content first, motion second.** Reveals and transitions support reading; they never compete with it. The page must be fully legible with motion off.
- **Resume-ready.** The resume is a real artifact people will want to print or save; the layout respects that.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG 2.1 AA. Body text must meet ≥4.5:1 contrast (watch `--color-text-secondary` on tinted backgrounds, which sits at the AA threshold). `prefers-reduced-motion` is honored: all reveal and hover motion has a reduced-motion fallback and content is never gated behind a transition. Semantic landmarks, a skip link, keyboard-navigable nav, and visible focus states are required.
